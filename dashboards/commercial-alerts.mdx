Commercial Alerts **monitor your account and flag when something important changes**. Instead of manually checking every metric each week, alerts surface the issues that need your attention, things like sudden sales drops, availability declines, or distribution losses.

## Why it matters

<Tabs>
  <Tab title="Account Managers">
    You can't watch every SKU every week. Alerts act as your **safety net**. They catch the things you'd miss in a busy week, a SKU quietly losing distribution, availability sliding on a key product, or a sudden sales drop that needs investigating before your buyer notices it first.
  </Tab>
  <Tab title="Supply Chain Analysts">
    Alerts give you **time to react**. A depot stock alert firing on Monday gives you days to chase a delivery or escalate before the gap hits store shelves. Without alerts, you're finding problems after they've already cost you sales.
  </Tab>
</Tabs>

## When to use it

| Trigger | What you're checking |
|---------|---------------------|
| **Start of the week** (Monday morning) | What fired over the weekend? Any new issues to deal with? |
| **Before reviewing dashboards** | Let alerts guide where you spend your time. Start with the fires |
| **After resolving an issue** | Has the alert cleared, or is the problem still active? |
| **Quarterly review** | Which alerts fire most often? These are your systemic issues |

## What to look for

<AccordionGroup>
  <Accordion title="Recurring alerts on the same SKU" icon="rotate">
    If the same product keeps triggering alerts week after week, you don't have an isolated incident. You have a **systemic problem** that needs a structural fix, whether that's a supply chain review, a ranging conversation, or a promotional rethink.
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="Multiple alert types firing at once" icon="bells">
    When sales, availability, and supply alerts all fire simultaneously for the same product, the **root cause is almost always supply**. Follow the chain: depot stock dropped, then availability dropped, then sales dropped. Fix the supply issue and the rest recovers.
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="Alert fired but metrics look fine now" icon="circle-check">
    This usually means the issue was **short-lived**, perhaps a single week blip in supply or a data lag. Check the trend over the last 3-4 weeks. If everything else looks stable, acknowledge it and move on. Not every alert requires action.
  </Accordion>
</AccordionGroup>

## How to read it

<Steps>
  <Step title="Review active alerts by severity">
    Start with the highest severity alerts. These represent the biggest commercial risk to your account.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Group by alert type">
    Are you seeing mostly sales alerts, availability alerts, or supply alerts? The pattern tells you where the underlying problem sits.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Check the affected products">
    Are alerts concentrated on a few key SKUs or spread across the range? Concentrated alerts mean a targeted fix. Spread alerts mean a broader issue.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Drill into the relevant dashboard">
    Use the alert as your starting point, then jump to the specific dashboard for detail. Sales alert? Go to the Sales Report. Availability alert? Go to the Availability Report.
  </Step>
</Steps>

## Quick reference

| Alert Type | What triggers it | What to do |
|-----------|-----------------|------------|
| **Sales Drop** | Sales value or volume falls significantly vs comparison period | Check Sales Report for which SKUs are driving the drop. Is it demand or availability? |
| **Availability Decline** | Availability % drops below threshold or falls sharply | Go to Availability Report. Check if the issue is supply or store replenishment |
| **Distribution Loss** | Stores ranged decreases for a product | Confirm with buyer whether this is a deliberate delist or an error. Act quickly if unintended |
| **Supply Alert** | DC inbound falls below threshold or depot stock reaches critical level | Check Depot Stock report. Chase delivery status with your supply chain team |
| **Rate of Sale Change** | Significant shift in rate of sale, up or down | Investigate in Sales Report. Rising ROS with flat sales could mean distribution loss |
| **Waste Spike** | Waste value or percentage increases sharply | Check if this is a shelf-life issue, over-supply, or a store execution problem |

<Tip>
  **Pro tip:** Don't try to action every alert. Prioritise by commercial impact. A high-severity alert on your number one SKU is worth more attention than a low-severity alert on a tail product. Use alerts to decide where to spend your time, not to create a to-do list of everything.
</Tip>
