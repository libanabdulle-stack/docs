## Why it matters

<Tabs>
  <Tab title="Account Managers">
    This is where you build your **buyer story**. When the Top Line Report flags a change, the Sales Report tells you which SKUs, stores, or time periods are driving it. You need this to walk into a meeting with specifics, not just "sales are down".
  </Tab>
  <Tab title="Category Controllers">
    This is your **range review preparation tool**. Compare sales velocity across SKUs to identify which products earn their shelf space and which ones are candidates for delisting or repositioning.
  </Tab>
</Tabs>

## When to use it

| Trigger | What you're checking |
|---------|---------------------|
| **After reviewing the Top Line Report** | Which specific SKUs are driving the overall trend? |
| **Range review preparation** | Sales velocity comparison across your product range |
| **Promotional review** | Did the promotion actually lift sales, or just pull forward demand? |
| **New product launch tracking** | Is the new SKU building momentum week on week? |

## What to look for

<AccordionGroup>
  <Accordion title="A small number of SKUs driving most of the change" icon="bullseye">
    This is common. If 2-3 SKUs account for most of the sales movement, focus your energy there. Check whether the change is driven by rate of sale (demand) or distribution (store count).
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="Sales growth in volume but not value" icon="scale-unbalanced">
    This suggests **price erosion or promotional dependency**. You're selling more units but generating less revenue per unit. Check whether promotional activity is cannibalising full-price sales.
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="Strong rate of sale but flat total sales" icon="equals">
    The product is performing well where it's stocked, but it's **not in enough stores**. This is your case for distribution expansion. Use the rate of sale figures to support a ranging request.
  </Accordion>
</AccordionGroup>

## How to read it

Follow this sequence to get from headline to insight:

<Steps>
  <Step title="Set your time period and comparison">
    Choose the period you're investigating and what you're comparing against. Year on year is best for spotting real trends. Period on period catches recent shifts.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Sort by sales value">
    Identify your top revenue drivers. These are the SKUs that matter most to your total number.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Check the variance column">
    Which products are growing and which are declining? Sort by variance to find the biggest movers in either direction.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Drill into the outliers">
    For any SKU with a significant change, ask: is this rate of sale (demand), distribution (store count), or both? The answer determines your next action.
  </Step>
</Steps>

## Dashboard controls

<CardGroup cols={2}>
  <Card title="Product Grouping" icon="layer-group">
    View by Category, Subcategory, Brand, or SKU. Start at category level to find the area of interest, then drill down to SKU.
  </Card>

## What's on screen

<CardGroup cols={3}>
  <Card title="Product Table" icon="table">
    The main panel. Every product row shows sales figures for the selected period with variance against comparison. Sortable by any column.
  </Card>
  <Card title="Variance Bars" icon="chart-bar">

  </Card>
</CardGroup>

## Quick reference

| Metric | Category | What it tells you |
|--------|----------|-------------------|
| **Sales Value** | Commercial | Total revenue for the selected period |
| **Sales Volume** | Commercial | Total units sold. Useful for spotting price vs volume effects |
| **Rate of Sale (Stores Ranged)** | Commercial | Units per store per week, across all stores that carry the product |
| **Rate of Sale (Stores Stocked)** | Commercial | Units per store per week, only counting stores with stock on shelf |
| **Average Price** | Commercial | Revenue divided by volume. Tracks pricing and promotional impact |
| **Stores Ranged** | Distribution | Number of stores authorised to carry the product |

<Tip>
  **Pro tip:** Compare Rate of Sale (Stores Ranged) against Rate of Sale (Stores Stocked). A big gap between the two means you have stores that are ranged but not actually stocking the product, which is an availability problem, not a demand problem.
</Tip>
